Intended Demographic Brainstorming

  • People who shop for personal groceries and/or produce
    • Adults
    • College students
  • People who shop for others
    • People paid to shop for other people
    • Small restaurants with changeable menus or seasonal dishes

Interview 1: college student

  • It's hard to make environmentally friendly choices
  • Lack of information at hand while at the store
  • Too busy for research beforehand
    • Also hard to know if you’re actually making the right choice because there’s so many different influences on the environmental impact of a single item
  • Lack of time to cook with fresh produce
    • Frozen food
    • Microwaveable meals, including a lot of disposable packaging
  • Balancing price, quality, and impact
  • Need to research recipes to use certain ingredients
  • Availability in stores / of stores

Interview 2: college student

  • Cosmetic issues: people don’t buy food that doesn’t look nice
  • Cheaper to buy in bulk, but hard to use it all up
    • Cheaper stuff runs a bigger risk of wasting it since it might go bad before you can use it

Interview 3: parent

  • Hard to find items in stores
    • Long isles, changing locations
  • Stickers on fruits and vegetables -- hard to remove and do not compost
  • Origins of fruit marked in very small font
  • Excessive packaging for things like berries
  • Limited variety of apples at safeway, not always good apples, too large
  • Problems with regulated words
    • "Local": not regulated
      • Often just means from somewhere in CA, not actually local to the area
    • "Organic": not regulated
    • USDA: somewhat regulated
      • Still not that great: only talks about what chickens eat, not how they are kept

User Research Summary

The interview process showed me different perspectives, especially about which factors and problems people thought were most important in the realm of grocery shopping. A lot of the problems dealt with lack of information in some form; there were problems with general knowledge, finding the time and sources to use for research, and having incomplete, badly designed, or otherwise unuseful information or labelling. For people concerned with the price of produce, a significant issue was the balance of price and environmental impact of the product. There were also more tangible problems related to price, such as buying in bulk to spend less money, but having the food go bad before having the opportunity to use it all. Previously, I was more oriented towards seasonality and geographic origins of produce, and how that knowledge could better inform consumers and their choice of produce. However, the people I interviewed enumerated some less abstract problems, though many still related to the information available to consumers. A lot of these problems are related to waste in some form, such as waste in the form of disposable packaging, excessive packaging, or food waste in stores and in homes. So, it would be relevant to broaden the scope of information I use, or perhaps shift my focus towards providing a more interactive, helpful project that could help people while they are actually at the store trying to make decisions, in addition to just at home. For example, I could maybe make an interactive guide to labelling systems, to make deciphering complex labels easier to understand and evaluate.
